# Layout Patterns for Slide Compositions

Recurring layout structures that work well in presentations.

## 1. Hero Statement
Full-screen text. One bold headline, centered. Maybe a subtitle.
Use for: opening slides, key messages, transitions.

## 2. Split Screen
Two equal columns. Left: text. Right: image/chart/graphic.
Use for: feature explanations, before/after, comparisons.

## 3. Three Column Grid
Three equal sections with icon/number + heading + body text.
Use for: process steps, feature highlights, service pillars.

## 4. Card Grid
2x2 or 2x3 grid of cards with consistent styling.
Use for: team members, product features, options comparison.
Max 6 cards per slide.

## 5. Full Bleed Image
Background image covering entire slide with text overlay.
Use for: emotional impact, product shots, team photos.
Ensure text has contrast (overlay, shadow, or solid panel).

## 6. Data Spotlight
Large chart/graph on one side, key metric callout on the other.
Use for: KPI presentations, quarterly reviews, data storytelling.

## 7. Quote Slide
Large quotation marks, big text, attribution below.
Use for: testimonials, thought leadership, emphasis.

## 8. Timeline
Horizontal or vertical timeline with milestones.
Use for: roadmaps, history, project plans.
Keep to 5-7 points maximum.

## 9. Comparison Table
Two columns, feature rows, checkmarks or highlights.
Use for: vs. competitor, before/after, option A vs B.

## 10. Section Divider
Full-screen with just a section number and title.
Use for: breaking the deck into chapters.
Provides visual breathing room.

## 11. Stats Row
3-4 large numbers with labels in a horizontal row.
Use for: key metrics, achievements, impact numbers.
Numbers should be the hero — use oversized type.

## 12. Process Flow
Sequential steps connected by arrows or lines.
Use for: workflows, methodologies, user journeys.
Keep to 4-6 steps.

---

## Composition Tips

- Asymmetry is more interesting than perfect center alignment
- Use negative space intentionally — it creates focus
- Vary layouts every 2-3 slides to maintain visual interest
- Align elements to a grid, even if the grid is not visible
- One focal point per slide — guide the eye
- Repeat visual patterns (same card style, same spacing) for cohesion
